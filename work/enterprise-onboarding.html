    From Chaos to Clarity: Redesigning Enterprise Onboarding

    New employees at Meridian HR's customers took 14 days on average to reach full productivity. I led a ground-up redesign of the onboarding experience — personalized by role, guided by context, backed by 40 user interviews — that cut time-to-productivity by 64%.

    My Role
    Lead UX Designer
    Timeline
    5 months (Q1–Q2 2024)
    Team
    2 Designers, 1 PM, 6 Engineers
    Tools
    Figma, UserTesting, Dovetail
    Overview

    Why onboarding was broken

    Meridian HR serves HR teams at companies with 500–5,000 employees. Its onboarding module is one of the platform's flagship features — but it had been built incrementally since 2018 and never redesigned holistically. By 2024, completion rates had stagnated at 43%, and the customer success team was spending significant time hand-holding new users through basic setup tasks.

    The core problem: the onboarding experience treated every employee identically, regardless of role, department, or seniority. A new software engineer and a new VP of Finance received the same 47-step checklist.

    14 daysAverage time for new employees to reach full productivity

    Research

    Listening at scale

    I conducted 40 user interviews across 8 customer organizations, speaking with new hires, hiring managers, and HR administrators. I supplemented this with a journey mapping workshop with 6 Meridian HR customers and a quantitative analysis of completion drop-off points in product analytics.

    The interviews surfaced a clear mental model mismatch: new hires thought about onboarding as "learning what I need to do my job," but the product presented it as "completing an administrative checklist." The most painful moments in the journey were when irrelevant tasks appeared — employees would hit a task that didn't apply to them, get confused, and abandon the flow entirely.

    "I didn't know if I was supposed to skip the tasks that didn't apply to me or if I was doing something wrong."

    — Research participant, new hire (Engineer, week 2)

    Design Process

    Designing for role, not for compliance

    The central design decision was to shift from a flat universal checklist to a role-adaptive flow. When a new hire is added to the platform, HR configures their role and department. The onboarding system then surfaces only the tasks relevant to that role — automatically filtered, sequenced by dependency, and grouped into meaningful milestones.

    I ran three design sprints over six weeks: one focused on the role-configuration admin interface (for HR teams), one on the employee-facing onboarding flow, and one on the contextual help and progress systems.

    • Role templates: HR admins can create onboarding templates per role family (Engineer, Finance, Sales, etc.) and assign them during the hiring workflow.
    • Progressive disclosure: Employees see the next 3 tasks at a time, not the full list. The full checklist is available but tucked behind a "See all" toggle to reduce overwhelm.
    • Contextual guidance: Each task has an inline explainer — short, plain-language instructions that replace the previous help-center redirect pattern.
    • Milestone celebrations: Every 5 completed tasks triggers a lightweight progress celebration — simple, but tested positively for motivation.

    Solution

    The redesigned experience

    The final design introduced a warm, focused onboarding home — a single page that greets the new hire by name, surfaces their first tasks with clear context, and shows an overall progress arc. The admin interface was rebuilt with a template system that reduced HR configuration time from 45 minutes to under 10 minutes per new hire cohort.
