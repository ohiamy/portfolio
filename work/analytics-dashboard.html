    Lumis Analytics · B2B SaaS · 2023

    Signal Over Noise: Redesigning a B2B Analytics Dashboard

    Power users were spending 8+ minutes completing core analytical tasks. I led a research-intensive redesign that introduced adaptive information architecture, progressive disclosure, and a saved-views system — cutting task time by 60%.

    My Role
    Lead UX Designer
    Timeline
    6 months (2023)
    Team
    1 Designer, 2 PMs, 8 Engineers
    Tools
    Figma, Maze, Hotjar, Looker
    Overview

    The problem

    Lumis Analytics is a B2B SaaS platform serving data teams at mid-market and enterprise companies. After a round of enterprise expansion, the customer success team flagged a troubling pattern: power users — the people who lived in the product daily — were churning at a higher rate than average users. Exit survey analysis revealed the culprit: the dashboard experience.

    Users described it as "overwhelming," "cluttered," and "slow to navigate." Hotjar recordings confirmed the feeling — users were spending 8–12 minutes per session completing tasks that should take under 2 minutes. The product had grown organically over four years, and the information architecture had never been revisited holistically.

    8 min Average time to complete a core analytical task (baseline)
    38% Power users citing UX as a reason for considering alternatives
    $1.8M ARR at risk from churning power-user accounts
    Research

    Understanding how power users think

    I designed a multi-method research plan to understand both the surface-level friction and the underlying mental models that weren't being served.

    • Task analysis sessions (n=18): Moderated sessions where I gave participants realistic tasks and observed their navigation patterns, hesitations, and workarounds.
    • Eye-tracking study (n=8): Partnered with an external research lab to run eye-tracking on key dashboard views. Results revealed 60% of visual attention was focused on chrome/navigation rather than data.
    • 6-week diary study (n=12): Participants submitted brief video reports of their daily Lumis usage. This surfaced recurring pain points invisible in moderated sessions.
    • Competitive audit: Analyzed 6 competing tools to identify dashboard patterns that were becoming user expectations.

    "I know the answer is in here somewhere. I just can't find it without clicking through five menus every time."

    — Participant, Task Analysis Session #4

    Key insight: power users weren't struggling because they didn't understand the product — they were experts. They were struggling because the interface forced them to re-navigate the same paths repeatedly, with no way to save their work context between sessions.

    Design Process

    From insight to system

    Research synthesis revealed three root causes: (1) a flat navigation structure that treated all features as equally important, (2) no persistent workspace concept — every session started from scratch, and (3) data density without hierarchy, making it hard to distinguish signal from noise.

    I ran a two-week design sprint with the PM team to generate concepts, then conducted four rounds of concept testing with power users before moving to high-fidelity.

    The key interventions

    • Adaptive navigation: Surfaces the user's most-used reports and views at the top level. Infrequently-used features are still accessible but tucked behind a single "More" menu.
    • Saved views system: Users can name and pin any configured dashboard state — filters, date ranges, chart types — and return to it in one click. This directly addressed the "re-navigate every time" pattern from research.
    • Progressive complexity: Default views show the most critical KPIs. Advanced breakdowns are available via expandable panels — visible when needed, hidden when not.
    • Keyboard navigation: Power users overwhelmingly preferred keyboard shortcuts. We shipped a command palette (Cmd+K) covering the 20 most common actions.

    Solution

    The redesigned dashboard

    The final design introduced a workspace metaphor — users now have a persistent context (their saved views and recently accessed reports) as the home base, instead of arriving at an empty dashboard each session.

    Navigation was restructured around job-to-be-done categories rather than feature categories, which matched the mental models surfaced in research much more closely.

    Outcomes

    Results after launch

    The redesign shipped in Q3 2023. We measured against baseline metrics from the research phase.

    −60% Task completion time (8 min → 3.2 min average)
    +45% Feature discovery rate for advanced analytics tools
    −12% Power-user churn in the 6 months post-launch

    The saved-views feature became the highest-rated capability in the next NPS survey cycle, with 84% of power users reporting it as "essential to my daily workflow." The command palette was adopted by 62% of active users within 90 days of launch.

    This project reinforced for me that the difference between an okay redesign and a transformative one is depth of research. The saved-views insight only surfaced during the diary study — a methodology I advocated for when the PM team initially pushed for a lighter research phase.
