    Orion SaaS · Series C · 2023

    One Language: Building a Design System Across 12 Teams

    Orion had 12 product teams, 3 codebases, and zero shared visual standards. I built a token-based design system from the ground up — reducing design-to-dev handoff time by 40% and achieving 100% team adoption within 6 months.

    My Role
    Design System Lead
    Timeline
    8 months (2023)
    Team
    3 Designers, 4 Engineers, 1 PM
    Tools
    Figma, Storybook, GitHub, Notion
    Overview

    The cost of no shared standard

    Orion raised a Series C in 2022 and doubled engineering headcount. With growth came fragmentation: each of 12 product teams was designing and building independently. A component audit revealed over 400 unique components across the product — but only 60 distinct design patterns. The rest was duplication, variation, and inconsistency.

    The business impact was significant: engineers were rebuilding the same UI components repeatedly, designers were spending hours pixel-matching across tools, and customers were experiencing jarring visual inconsistencies as they moved between product areas.

    Discovery

    Understanding the ecosystem

    Before designing anything, I ran a 3-week discovery phase: 24 stakeholder interviews across design, engineering, and product leadership; a full component audit using a custom Figma plugin; and a survey of all 12 teams to understand their current workflow pain points.

    The interviews revealed that adoption would be the hardest challenge — not the system itself. Teams had tried to rally around shared standards before and failed. The trust had to be rebuilt, and the system had to demonstrably make their work easier, not just tell them to use it.

    "Every time someone proposes a shared component library, it dies because it doesn't match what we actually need. We've learned not to trust it."

    — Senior Engineer, Orion Growth team
    Design Process

    Building trust, not just components

    I took a federated model rather than a centralized mandate. The design system team (myself + 2 engineers) built the foundation — tokens, core components, documentation — but each product team had a designated "system ambassador" who sat in on design reviews and contributed team-specific patterns back to the library.

    • Design tokens first: We started with a complete token set (color, typography, spacing, radius, elevation) before touching a single component. This gave teams immediate value — they could adopt tokens even before the component library was ready.
    • Figma library + Storybook parity: Every component existed in both Figma and code simultaneously. We enforced this with a PR requirement: no Figma updates without a corresponding Storybook PR, and vice versa.
    • Documentation as a first-class deliverable: Each component had a dedicated doc page: usage guidelines, do/don't examples, prop documentation, and accessibility notes. No component shipped without it.
    • Pilot team approach: We launched with 2 willing teams, incorporated their feedback for 6 weeks, then expanded to the full 12. This built advocates before the mandate.

    Outcomes

    Results at 6 months

    100%Team adoption across all 12 product teams within 6 months
    −40%Design-to-dev handoff time (measured via project tracking)
    340+Duplicate components eliminated from the product codebase

    The system now has 87 components, 1,200+ token definitions, and a contribution guide that enables product teams to propose and ship new components independently. The federated model created a sense of shared ownership — rather than the system being something imposed on teams, it became something teams actively contribute to.
