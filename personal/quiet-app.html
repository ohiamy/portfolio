    The Quiet App: Designing for Neurodivergent Focus

    After my own ADHD diagnosis, I started noticing how most productivity apps pile on visual stimulation at exactly the wrong moment. The Quiet App is a focus tool designed from the ground up for minds that find noise — visual or otherwise — overwhelming.

    Type
    Personal / Open Source
    Timeline
    12 weeks (2022)
    Methods
    Participatory Design, Iterative Prototyping
    Tools
    Figma, Maze, Notion
    Context

    Starting from my own experience

    I was diagnosed with ADHD at 31. Looking back at every productivity system I'd tried — Pomodoro timers, task apps, habit trackers — they all shared a common problem: they were designed to motivate neurotypical users through streak counters, badges, progress bars, and notification systems. For me, those elements were distraction mechanisms, not motivation mechanisms.

    I started designing The Quiet App as a personal project, then opened it up. I recruited 8 people with ADHD from online communities to participate in the research and design process — turning a personal project into a participatory one.

    Research

    What ADHD users actually need from a focus tool

    I combined desk research (academic literature on ADHD and attention, UX writing on cognitive load) with 8 in-depth interviews with my participant group. I also ran 2-week diary studies with 4 participants, asking them to track when and why their existing tools failed them.

    The core insight: the biggest barrier to focus for ADHD users isn't the absence of motivation — it's the presence of competing stimuli. A productivity app that shows me 47 tasks is an attention catastrophe, not a tool.

    "Every time I open my task app to find the one thing I should be doing, I get distracted by 12 other things I'm not doing. Then I feel bad and don't do anything."

    — Research participant
    Design Decisions

    Principles that drove every choice

    • One thing at a time: The app shows exactly one task — the one you've nominated as your current focus. Everything else is hidden until you ask for it.
    • No gamification: No streaks, no badges, no leaderboards. The only feedback is "done" or "not done." Removing gamification removed the anxiety loop it created for participants.
    • Low visual density: Maximum contrast between the focus item and the background. No color, no icons, no competing visual elements in the focus view.
    • Flexible timers: Rather than a fixed 25-minute Pomodoro, users set their own "I can handle this much" duration — even if that's 5 minutes. No guilt about short sessions.
    • Soft interruptions: Notifications are off by default. When a session ends, the transition is gentle — no alarms, no urgency cues.

    Outcomes

    What happened after I shared it

    I published the Figma file to Figma Community with a write-up of the design rationale. Within 6 months, it had been downloaded over 1,200 times. The comments section became a small community of ADHD designers and developers sharing their own variations.

    Three developers have built partial implementations of the concept. I'm not planning to build it myself — this project was about the design process and giving something useful back to a community I belong to.
