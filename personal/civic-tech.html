    Neighborhood as Network: Speculative Civic Tech

    An 8-week self-initiated design sprint exploring what local civic participation could look like if all its fragmented touchpoints — voting, service requests, neighborhood alerts, community forums — lived in one coherent experience.

    Type
    Speculative / Self-initiated
    Timeline
    8 weeks (2023)
    Methods
    Co-design, Secondary Research, Prototyping
    Tools
    Figma, Miro, Notion
    The Framing

    Why civic participation is so low

    Less than 10% of residents in most U.S. cities vote in local elections — the ones that have the most direct impact on their daily lives. The issue isn't apathy; it's fragmentation. Reporting a pothole happens on one app. Attending a neighborhood meeting requires a different sign-up. Tracking a city council vote happens on a website built in 2009.

    I wanted to explore: what if there was a single "civic layer" for your neighborhood — a platform that unified all the touchpoints of local participation into one coherent, low-friction experience?

    Approach

    Co-design with real neighbors

    I recruited 3 neighborhood groups in my city: a local block association, a parent group from an elementary school, and a tenants' union. I ran 2-hour co-design sessions with each group — using mapping exercises to surface what civic participation meant to them and where the biggest friction points were.

    Secondary research covered existing platforms (Nextdoor, SeeClickFix, Decidim) and academic literature on civic technology. The gap was clear: most tools optimize for reporting or consuming information, not for sustained participation and relationship-building.

    "I want to know what's happening on my block, not what's happening in the whole city. I want to feel like my input actually goes somewhere."

    — Co-design participant, block association member
    Design Direction

    A neighborhood-first architecture

    The concept centered on a "neighborhood radius" — a default view scoped to the 6-block radius around your home address. Everything is hyperlocal first, with optional expansion to the city level. The three core surfaces were:

    • The Feed: A stream of neighborhood events, service requests, community proposals, and local meeting notes — all contributed or verified by residents and civic staff alike.
    • The Map: A visual layer showing open service requests, recent repairs, planned construction, and community resources — pulling from city open data APIs.
    • Your Voice: A participatory budgeting-style interface where residents can propose, discuss, and vote on hyperlocal improvements — with a live tracker showing how their input influenced a decision.

    Outcomes

    What came next

    I tested the prototype with 22 residents across the three co-design groups. The "Your Voice" feature had the strongest response — participants described it as "the first time I could imagine my input actually mattering." The Map view resonated with parents and older residents; the Feed was most valued by people new to the neighborhood.

    I submitted the concept to my city's civic innovation lab and was invited to present at a local civic tech meetup. The project is open — if you're working on participatory civic tools, I'd love to connect.
